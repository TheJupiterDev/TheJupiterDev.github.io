Why I Code and Write

April 25, 2025

Most people see writing and coding as opposites.

One’s creative, emotional, and messy. The other is logical, structured, and precise.

But for me, they’ve always gone together. I don’t choose between them — I chase both.

Because whether I’m building a story or a script, I’m doing the same thing:
I’m creating something from nothing.

✍️ Writing with Meaning

I’ve been writing stories for as long as I can remember.

The Echoverse — my fictional universe — didn’t just appear one day. It started with half-finished characters, scattered lore, and late-night scribbles in notebooks. (Not even joking — the first thing I ever wrote down for the Echoverse was a one-line note about Kael, in the dark, on a scrap of paper.)

Over time, those pieces became worlds, timelines, and sagas — each one tied to the others. And while none of it’s finished yet, I love the process of watching raw ideas turn into living stories.

Writing gives me the freedom to wonder, wander, and ask “what if?” What if this character broke the rules? What if this war happened again? What if magic had a cost?

There’s something magical (pun fully intended) about turning a blank page into a place someone could get lost in.

💻 Coding with Precision

I started coding when I was 8 — Python was my love language.

Since then I’ve played with other languages, built desktop apps, made websites, and created tools like mDirt — a PySide6-powered app that helps generate Minecraft datapacks. (It started as a practice project, but became something genuinely helpful. Funny how that happens.)

Coding scratches a different itch than writing. It’s logical, structured, and satisfying in a way writing isn’t. Solving a bug that has been haunting you for hours is the most rewarding feeling one can get.

And yes, sometimes it’s frustrating (especially when you see "Traceback, most recent call last" pop up on the console for the umpteenth time). But when it works — when you finally fix that thing — it feels great.

🔁 Where They Intersect

You’d think writing and coding would live on opposite sides of my brain — but honestly? They overlap more than people expect.

  • Creativity — for dreaming up new solutions, or characters
  • Structure — to organize a story, or a function
  • Patience — because nothing ever works the first time (trust me)
  • Revision — writing needs editing; so does code

I’ve rebuilt mDirt at least three times. I’ve rewritten book plots just as often. Planning code and planning a novel aren’t that different: you break it into parts, debug when stuff breaks, and refactor the pacing.

Okay, maybe I don’t really “debug” character arcs. But you get the idea.

☕ Why I’ll Keep Doing Both

Some days, I write.

Some days, I code.

Most days, I drink coffee and try to do both.

And I love that balance — logic and creativity, structure and chaos. It keeps me grounded, curious, and always learning something new.

So whether I’m writing a scene or a script, plotting a kingdom’s fall or a function’s return value — I’m doing the same thing: creating something that didn’t exist before.

And honestly? That’s the part I’ll never stop loving.

Oh — and coding will probably make me more money than writing. 😂

— Joel
